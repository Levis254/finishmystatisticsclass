What Is the Cincinnati Seasonings SCM Globe Simulation?

The Cincinnati Seasonings case study is a supply chain simulation where a company manufactures and distributes spice blends from a distribution center in Cincinnati to multiple retail stores.

The objective is to design and manage a supply chain that can operate successfully over a specified period while minimizing costs and maintaining product availability.

Students are required to make decisions regarding:

  • Inventory levels
  • Transportation routes
  • Vehicle selection
  • Production rates
  • Distribution center operations
  • Store replenishment policies

The challenge is that changes in one area often affect every other part of the supply chain.

Common Problems Students Face

Many students encounter issues such as:

Inventory Overflow

A common mistake is maintaining excessive inventory at stores and distribution centers. While this may reduce stockout risk, it significantly increases storage and operational costs.

Stockouts

Reducing inventory too aggressively can result in stockouts, causing the simulation to fail before reaching the target completion period.

Transportation Inefficiencies

Using dedicated trucks for every location often leads to excessive transportation costs and underutilized vehicle capacity.

Poor Profitability

Even when the simulation runs successfully, students frequently struggle to achieve positive profits because operating expenses remain too high.

Key Strategies for Improving Performance

Align Production with Demand

One of the first areas to examine is factory production. Many simulations begin with production levels that exceed actual demand. Excess production creates unnecessary inventory accumulation throughout the supply chain.

Instead, production should closely match expected demand while maintaining sufficient flexibility to avoid shortages.

Reduce Inventory Without Creating Stockouts

Successful supply chain design requires balancing inventory carrying costs with service levels. Holding less inventory reduces storage expenses, facility operating costs, and working capital requirements. However, inventory reductions should be supported by carefully planned replenishment schedules.

Optimize Transportation Routes

Transportation often represents one of the largest cost categories within the simulation. Instead of assigning separate vehicles to every store, consider opportunities for:

  • Multi-stop delivery routes
  • Route consolidation
  • Higher vehicle utilization
  • Alternative transportation modes

Efficient routing can dramatically improve profitability.

Evaluate Rail Transportation

One of the most overlooked opportunities in the Cincinnati Seasonings simulation is rail transportation. Depending on the network design, rail can offer:

  • Lower transportation costs
  • Higher carrying capacity
  • Reduced carbon emissions
  • Improved overall efficiency

Students who evaluate multiple transportation options often achieve stronger results than those who rely solely on trucks.

Understanding the Inventory-Transportation Trade-Off

One of the most important lessons from the Cincinnati Seasonings simulation is that inventory and transportation costs are interconnected.

Reducing inventory usually requires more frequent deliveries. Increasing delivery frequency may raise transportation costs.

The objective is not to minimize a single cost category but to minimize the total supply chain cost while maintaining acceptable service levels. This concept appears repeatedly in real-world supply chain management and is one of the primary learning objectives of the simulation.

Writing the Final Report

Many students focus entirely on obtaining a successful simulation run and overlook the report component. A strong report should explain:

  • Why specific decisions were made
  • How inventory levels were optimized
  • How transportation routes were redesigned
  • The impact of those changes on costs and profitability
  • Lessons learned from the simulation

Instructors are typically interested in the reasoning behind your decisions, not just the final results.
